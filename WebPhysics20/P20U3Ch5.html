5.2 Circular Motion and Newton’s Laws

The velocity of circular motion can be determined by dividing the circumference by the period.
The centripetal acceleration of an object is determined by the velocity squared divided by the radius.
Newton’s second law states and can be applied to centripetal acceleration. A car making a turn experiences a centripetal acceleration and force that is created by the force of friction between the tires and the road.
The minimum speed necessary to move an object through a vertical loop equates centripetal force with gravitational force. Centripetal force can be equated to the gravitational force for planetary objects.
Centripetal acceleration and force can be determined using period and frequency instead of speed.

Cycle – one complete back-and-forth motion or oscillation
Revolution – one complete cycle for an object moving in a circular path
Period – the time required for an object to make one complete oscillation (cycle)
Frequency – the number of cycles per second measured in hertz (Hz)
RPM – revolutions per minute


 


5.3 Satellites and Celestial Bodies in Circular Motion

Kepler formulated three laws that explained the motion of planets in the solar system.
  1. All planets in the solar system have elliptical orbits with the Sun at one focus
  2. A line drawn from the Sun to a planet sweeps out equal areas in equal times.
  3. The ratio of a planet’s orbital period squared to its orbital radius cubed is a constant. All the objects orbiting the same focus (e.g. planets, the sun) have the same constant.
