Unit D: Atomic Physics

Themes: Energy and Matter
Overview: In this unit, students study the development and modification of models of the structure of matter.
This unit builds on:
  • • Science 9, Unit E: Space Exploration
  • • Physics 20, Unit C: Circular Motion, Work and Energy
  • • Physics 30, Unit A: Momentum and Impulse, Unit B: Forces and Fields and Unit C: Electromagnetic Radiation
This unit prepares students for further study of the nature of matter in post-secondary studies in physics. Unit D will require approximately 25% of the time allotted for Physics 30.

Focusing Questions:
  • How is the atom organized?
  • How can a model of(SI the atom and the nucleus be subjected to experimental tests?
  • How does knowledge of the internal structure of the atom lead to the development of applications of energy supply and to the technology of diagnostic imaging?
General Outcomes: There are four major outcomes in this unit.
Students will:
  1. 1. describe the electrical nature of the atom
  2. 2. describe the quantization of energy in atoms and nuclei
  3. 3. describe nuclear fission and fusion as powerful energy sources in nature
  4. 4. describe the ongoing development of models of the structure of matter.
Key Concepts: The following concepts are developed in this unit and may also be addressed in other units or in other courses. The intended level and scope of treatment is defined by the learning outcomes.
  • • charge-to-mass ratio (Thomson’s experiment)
  • • classical model of the atom (Rutherford, Bohr)
  • • spectra: continuous, line emission and line absorption
  • • energy levels (states)
  • • de Broglie hypothesis
  • • quantum mechanical model
  • • half-life
  • • nuclear decay
  • • nuclear reactions
  • • Standard Model of matter

16.1 The Nucleus

The nucleus is made up of a positively charged proton and a neutron with no electrical charge.
It is held together by the strong nuclear force.

Isotope – an atom with a different number of neutrons than normal.
It can be lighter or heavier. The mass given on the periodic table is a weighted average of all the different isotopes based on their frequency.
In1905 Albert Einstein showed that mass and energy are equivalent.
The mass defect (change) is measured in atomic mass units.
1 u = 1.66×10-27 kg
By comparing the mass of the nucleus to the mass of the nucleons, physicist found that the mass of the nucleus was lower than expected.
The nucleus is very stable because it has lost mass as energy. It requires large amounts of energy to cause a reaction within the nucleus and break the nucleons apart.
The more binding energy (lost mass) the nucleus has, the more stable the nucleus is (in nuclear reaction terms).


 


16.2 Radioactive Decay

Antoine Henri Becquerel discovered that compounds of uranium radiate a form of energy and cause photographic plates to react.
Marie and Pierre Curie studied this radiate energy and called it radiation. The amount of radiation was dependent only on the mass of the uranium. Therefore it had to be caused by the nucleus of the atom.
It is not affected by temperature.
Marie coined the term radioactive.
There are three forms of nuclear radiation:
  • Alpha α
    • the emission of a helium nucleus
    • stopped by approx. 10 cm air
    • stopped by paper
    • not dangerous unless ingested, then it is extremely dangerous

  • Beta particle β
    • emission of a high energy electron
    • stopped by few m of air
    • stopped by Al foil
    • penetrates outer layers of skin
    • medium danger

  • Gamma ray γ
    • emission of high frequency EMR
    • stopped by several cm of lead shielding

Potential Hazards of Nuclear Radiation
Radiation Sickness – radiation can ionize cellular material (cause an electrical charge). This disrupts the body’s biochemistry, and can kill vital organ cells. Can be minor or fatal depending on the dose of radiation.
Genetic Damage – High energy particles and gamma rays can alter DNA and cause mutation (cancer). These can show up 10 to 15 years later.
