Unit D: Atomic Physics

Themes: Energy and Matter
Overview: In this unit, students study the development and modification of models of the structure of matter.
This unit builds on:
  • • Science 9, Unit E: Space Exploration
  • • Physics 20, Unit C: Circular Motion, Work and Energy
  • • Physics 30, Unit A: Momentum and Impulse, Unit B: Forces and Fields and Unit C: Electromagnetic Radiation
This unit prepares students for further study of the nature of matter in post-secondary studies in physics. Unit D will require approximately 25% of the time allotted for Physics 30.

Focusing Questions:
  • How is the atom organized?
  • How can a model of(SI the atom and the nucleus be subjected to experimental tests?
  • How does knowledge of the internal structure of the atom lead to the development of applications of energy supply and to the technology of diagnostic imaging?
General Outcomes: There are four major outcomes in this unit.
Students will:
  1. 1. describe the electrical nature of the atom
  2. 2. describe the quantization of energy in atoms and nuclei
  3. 3. describe nuclear fission and fusion as powerful energy sources in nature
  4. 4. describe the ongoing development of models of the structure of matter.
Key Concepts: The following concepts are developed in this unit and may also be addressed in other units or in other courses. The intended level and scope of treatment is defined by the learning outcomes.
  • • charge-to-mass ratio (Thomson’s experiment)
  • • classical model of the atom (Rutherford, Bohr)
  • • spectra: continuous, line emission and line absorption
  • • energy levels (states)
  • • de Broglie hypothesis
  • • quantum mechanical model
  • • half-life
  • • nuclear decay
  • • nuclear reactions
  • • Standard Model of matter

17.1 Detecting and Measuring Subatomic Particles

Cloud and Bubble Chambers
Both show the path of particles traveling through as the particles energy causes a trail through the gas in the chamber. By applying a magnetic field across the chamber the particle’s path curves allowing measurements to be taken.


 


17.2 Quantum Theory and The Discovery of New Particles

In 1928 Paul Dirac combined Einstein’s theory of relativity and Schrodinger’s wave equations of the atom and predicted the existence of antimatter. Antimatter is matter with an opposite charge (electron that is positive, positron)
In 1932 Carl Anderson photographed the first proof of antimatter. It was a particle with the charge to mass ratio of an electron but curved the opposite direction in a magnetic field.
Antimatter is the same as regular matter except that it has the opposite electric charge.
Matter plus antimatter changes mass into energy.
Quantum Field Theory
Forces: electrostatics, gravity, weak nuclear and strong nuclear force are created by the exchange of mediating particles (virtual). These virtual particles travel back and forth between the involved actual particles.


 


17.3 Probing the Structure of Matter

Subatomic particles (smaller than proton and neutron) are groups of particles found only in particle accelerators and are being researched to determine how they interact.
They are described by their mass as energy per speed of light squared.

17.4 Quarks and the Standard Model

The Quark Model
